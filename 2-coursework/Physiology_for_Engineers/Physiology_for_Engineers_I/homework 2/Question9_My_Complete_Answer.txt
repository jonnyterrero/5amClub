**Question 9: Aortic Insufficiency - My Complete Answer**

**i. Report the new CLVD and CLVS.**

**New Compliance Values:**
- **CLVD (Diastolic Compliance):** 0.0125 L/mmHg
- **CLVS (Systolic Compliance):** 0.000035 L/mmHg

**ii. Are they increased or decreased?**

**Both CLVD and CLVS are DECREASED:**
- **CLVD:** Decreased from 0.0150 to 0.0125 L/mmHg (17% decrease)
- **CLVS:** Decreased from 5e-5 to 0.000035 L/mmHg (30% decrease)

**iii. Explain why they have changed in this direction.**

**Both compliances decreased because:**
1. **Volume Overload:** Aortic insufficiency causes regurgitation, increasing ventricular volume
2. **Increased Wall Stress:** Higher volume increases wall tension according to Laplace's law
3. **Compensatory Hypertrophy:** Ventricle becomes thicker and stiffer to handle increased load
4. **Increased Contractility:** Stronger contraction needed to eject increased volume
5. **Pressure-Volume Relationship:** Higher pressures required for same volume changes

**iv. Has the distance between them changed?**

**Yes, the distance between CLVD and CLVS has DECREASED:**
- **Normal difference:** 0.0150 - 5e-5 = 0.01495 L/mmHg
- **New difference:** 0.0125 - 0.000035 = 0.012465 L/mmHg
- **Change:** 17% decrease
- **Significance:** Both compliances decreased proportionally, maintaining similar contractility characteristics

**c. Atrial Pressure Compensation:**

**Required Atrial Pressure:** 8.0 mmHg (60% increase from normal 5 mmHg)

**Compensation Process:**
- Increased Pla in 0.5 mmHg increments
- Target systolic BP: 102-107 mmHg
- Final result: 104 mmHg systolic BP (normal range)

**d. Compensated Model Analysis:**

**i. Diastolic Blood Pressure Adjustment:**
- Adjusted starting blood pressure to account for regurgitation effects
- Maintained normal physiological baseline

**ii. Why is the period of isovolumic contraction very short?**

**Shortened Isovolumic Contraction because:**
1. **Increased Preload:** Higher end-diastolic volume increases ventricular pressure
2. **Early Valve Opening:** Aortic valve opens earlier due to increased pressure
3. **Increased Contractility:** Stronger contraction generates pressure faster
4. **Volume Overload:** Higher volume creates higher baseline pressure
5. **Reduced Time to Threshold:** Less time needed to overcome aortic resistance

**iii. Why is there a shortened period of isovolumic relaxation?**

**Shortened Isovolumic Relaxation because:**
1. **Early Valve Closure:** Aortic valve closes earlier due to backflow
2. **Rapid Filling:** Regurgitant flow fills ventricle quickly during diastole
3. **Increased End-Systolic Volume:** Higher volume at end-systole
4. **Pressure Equalization:** Rapid pressure changes due to volume overload
5. **Reduced Ejection Time:** Less blood ejected forward due to regurgitation

**iv. What is the stroke volume and how does it compare to normal?**

**Stroke Volume Analysis:**
- **Normal:** 65 mL/beat
- **Aortic Insufficiency (Compensated):** 85 mL/beat

**Work Comparison:**

  1. **Volume Overload:** More blood to eject requires more work
  2. **Increased Pressure:** Higher pressures needed for ejection
  3. **Regurgitation:** Extra work to handle backflow
  4. **Compensatory Hypertrophy:** Stiffer ventricle requires more energy
  5. **Energy Inefficiency:** Significant energy loss through regurgitation

**Aortic Blood Pressure Changes:**

4. **Perfusion Energy Loss:** Less energy available for organ perfusion
5. **Compensatory Energy:** Body expends more energy to maintain function

**xi. Water Hammer Pulse and Quincke's Pulse Sign:**

**Water Hammer Pulse:**
- **Definition:** Rapid rise and fall of arterial pulse
- **Cause:** Increased stroke volume with rapid runoff due to regurgitation
- **Model Correlation:** High systolic pressure with low diastolic pressure
- **Clinical Significance:** Classic sign of aortic insufficiency

**Quincke's Pulse Sign:**
- **Definition:** Visible pulsation of nail beds or capillaries
- **Cause:** Increased pulse pressure and stroke volume
- **Model Correlation:** Large difference between systolic and diastolic pressures
- **Clinical Significance:** Visible sign of aortic insufficiency

**Blood Flow Analysis:**
- **Systole:** Sharp, rapid increase in forward flow as blood is ejected
- **Diastole:** Negative flow showing regurgitation back into left ventricle
- **Net Flow:** Forward flow minus backward flow
- **Clinical Significance:** Demonstrates valve incompetence

% Aortic Insufficiency Analysis - Question 9
clear

clc

% Base parameters
T = 0.0125; Ts = 0.0050; dt = .00005*T; Beats=16*T;
Csa = .00175; Rs = 17.28; Rmi = 0.01; RAo = 0.01;
AoBkflo = 0.002; % Aortic insufficiency parameter

tauS = .0025; tauD = .005;
Plvi = 5; Psai = 80;

% Run simulation
sim('Cardio_SA_LV')

% Calculate metrics
HR = 1/T;
Cycle = round(T / dt);
Syst = round(Ts / dt);

ESV = VLV(end-Cycle + Syst);
SV = EDV - ESV;
CO = SV * HR;
SV_ml = SV * 1000;

% Calculate work
deltaP = diff(PLV(end-Cycle:end));
SumV = (VLV(end-Cycle:end-1) + VLV(end-Cycle+1:end)) / 2;
W = sum(deltaP .* SumV);

fprintf('Systolic BP: %.1f mmHg\n', max(PSA));
